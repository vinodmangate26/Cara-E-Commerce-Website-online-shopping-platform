How to Style a Quiff

The quiff has been a symbol of confidence and personal style since the 1950s, and it shows no signs of slowing down. What started as a rock-and-roll rebellion against conformity has evolved into one of the most adaptable and enduring hairstyles in men's grooming. Whether you lean classic or contemporary, the quiff has a version for you.

Understanding the Quiff

At its core, the quiff is defined by volume at the front of the head — hair brushed upward and slightly back, creating a peak or wave above the forehead. The sides are typically shorter, either faded or cut close, which makes the top volume the clear focal point. It's architectural in its logic: clean geometry framing a bold centrepiece.

"A great quiff isn't about perfection — it's about intention. Own it, and it works."

Products You'll Need

The right product is everything. For a modern, natural-finish quiff, a matte clay or paste gives you workability and hold without the stiffness of older pomades. If you're going for the classic slicked-back look, a light pomade with medium shine works best. Apply to damp hair before blow-drying for maximum volume and control.

For fine hair, a volumising mousse applied at the roots before heat-styling adds the lift that thicker hair achieves naturally. Don't skip the blow-dryer — it's your primary shaping tool. Use a medium-barrel brush and direct the airflow from root to tip while lifting the hair upward.

Step-by-Step: The Modern Quiff

Start by washing and towel-drying your hair until it's about 70% dry. Apply a small amount of styling clay — a pea-sized amount is usually enough — working it through from mid-lengths to ends. Then, using your blow-dryer on medium heat, brush the front section upward and slightly back. Once dry, use your fingers to refine the shape, then finish with a light-hold hairspray to lock everything in place without killing the movement.

Keeping It Fresh

The quiff rewards regular trims — every 4 to 6 weeks keeps the sides tight and the top at the right length for styling. Talk to your barber about the fade you want: a skin fade gives maximum contrast and a sharper look, while a scissor cut on the sides keeps things softer and more relaxed. Both work — it comes down to the overall aesthetic you're going for.
